The era of generic product scores is over

You're standing in a grocery aisle. You pull out your phone, wrestle with a crinkled barcode on a protein bar, and finally get a scan. The app shows a glowing green score. You buy it. You eat it. You feel terrible because the bar is packed with whey, and you're lactose intolerant. The app never asked, and it never knew.

For years, apps like Yuka, Open Food Facts, and Think Dirty have been our digital bodyguards in stores and bathrooms. They've done solid work highlighting artificial dyes, questionable preservatives, and hidden sugars. But they all share a fundamental flaw: they assume every human body is the same.

"Healthy for the average person" and "healthy for you" are two wildly different things. That's where Gud For Us enters and changes the game entirely.

The core problem

Legacy scanning apps rate products in a vacuum. A chocolate bar gets scored on sugar and fat content but nobody asks if you have a peanut allergy, diabetes, or sensitive skin. The score means nothing without context.

Why barcode-first apps are losing users

Barcode scanning apps created the product-transparency category. That's worth celebrating. But they have three persistent limitations that drive users away:

Friction at the shelf. You have to find the barcode, hold your phone steady, align it in low light, and hope it scans. Curved bottles, damaged labels, or missing barcodes break the experience entirely. Every failed scan is a moment your user considers deleting the app. And even when the scan succeeds, shoppers still have to decode the ingredient language itself, which is why our explainer on why ingredient lists are hard to understand is such a useful companion to this comparison.

The database gap. If a product isn't in the database, you're prompted to add it manually type the name, enter ingredients, upload a photo. Most people won't. They'll just put the product back and move on. The app becomes useless precisely when you need it most.

One-size-fits-all advice. A generic "72 out of 100" score tells you almost nothing about how a product interacts with your body. It doesn't know about your peanut allergy, your eczema, your diabetes, or the fact that you're pregnant. It's a number without a soul. If you want to see how risky that can become in practice, our guide on how to check if a product is safe for your allergy shows exactly where generic scoring breaks down.
